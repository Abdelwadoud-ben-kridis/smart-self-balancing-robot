01 · About the project

A balancing act,
engineered from scratch.

SBR-01 is a two-wheeled self-balancing robot designed and built end-to-end — mechanics, electronics, firmware and vision. An ESP32 reads an MPU-6500 IMU and runs a PID control loop at 100 Hz, correcting the robot's tilt many times per second to keep it upright on two wheels.

Everything sits on a custom 4-layer PCB carrying 40 components, designed in KiCad. A pair of articulated legs lets the robot change stance — standing tall or sitting low — while a Raspberry Pi 5 rides on top running computer vision, giving SBR-01 eyes as well as balance.

  • ESP32 real-time control @ 100 Hz
  • MPU-6500 6-axis IMU fusion
  • Custom 4-layer KiCad PCB
  • Articulated leg geometry
  • Raspberry Pi 5 computer vision
  • Fully 3D-printed structure

02 · Hardware

What's inside.

Every subsystem was chosen — and wired — for one job: keeping 18×15 cm of robot upright and aware.

ESP32-WROOM-32D

×1 · Real-time brain

Dual-core MCU running the 100 Hz balance loop, encoder counting, and motor PWM — hard real-time, no OS in the way.

Custom 4-Layer PCB

×1 · KiCad, 40 components

Power, drivers, sensors and MCU integrated on one board — no jumper-wire chaos, clean ground planes, 0 DRC violations.

L298N Motor Drivers

×2 · Dual H-bridge

Two dual H-bridges deliver bidirectional PWM drive to all four motors, with current headroom for balance corrections.

JGA25-370 DC Motors

×4 · With encoders

Geared DC motors with quadrature encoders — closed-loop speed feedback so the PID knows exactly how the wheels respond.

VL53L0X ToF Sensors

×4 · Laser ranging

Four time-of-flight laser sensors give near-360° proximity awareness for obstacle detection and safe navigation.

MPU9250 IMU

×1 · 9-axis motion

Accelerometer + gyro fused into a clean tilt angle — the single number the whole balancing act depends on.

Raspberry Pi 5

×1 · Vision computer

Runs the computer-vision stack and high-level behavior, talking to the ESP32 over serial while the ESP32 keeps balance.

04 · How it works

The 10-millisecond balancing act.

One hundred times per second, the robot senses its tilt, computes a correction, and pushes it to the wheels. Miss a few cycles and it falls over — that's the whole game.

Step 01 · Sense

IMU reads tilt

The MPU-6500 samples acceleration and angular rate; sensor fusion turns the raw data into a precise pitch angle.

θ = fuse(accel, gyro)
Step 02 · Compute

PID computes correction

The ESP32 compares the tilt against vertical and blends proportional, integral and derivative terms into one command.

u = Kp·e + Ki·∫e + Kd·ė
Step 03 · Act

Motors adjust speed

The L298N drivers translate the command into wheel torque — driving into the fall to catch it before it happens.

PWM → torque → balance
repeat · every 10 ms · 100 Hz
u(t) = Kp·e(t) + Ki·∫e(τ)dτ + Kd·de/dt
The PID control law — where e(t) is the tilt error between the robot and vertical.
